### what is biological essentialism? 
<hr>
Biological essentialism is the belief that biological characteristics constitute an innate essence that determine a personâ€™s traits, including complex social behaviors and life choices. As children learn about the world, they develop essentialist beliefs as a normal part of cognitive development. These beliefs can last through adulthood and may interact with a person's conceptual understanding of the world, including their perspective on ideological topics. 

Historically, essentialist philosophy is common among biologists. Before more recent developments in our understanding of biology, some believed that genetics predicted the ability, behavior, and merit of a person. Now, scholarly consensus among biologists illustrates that this idea is not based on evidence: living systems do not act deterministically and are directly affected by environmental factors. 

### why should biology instructors care?
<hr>
Biologically essentialist beliefs create pedagogical problems in the biology classroom. Students who hold these beliefs develop misunderstandings of complex biology concepts across topics in ecology, evolution, genetics, biochemistry, and physiology. These misunderstandings can lead to difficulties with a student's learning and performance in class, or in their future careers. In addition to harming our student's technical understanding of complex topics, these beliefs are also obstacles to organizational inclusion and retention goals. People who believe in essentialist explanations for human differences are more likely to believe harmful stereotypes, including about themselves.

Pedagogical choices within biology classes can reduce or reinforce biologically essentialist beliefs in students, and recent scholarly efforts have worked toward the development and validation of curricular frameworks that systematize the process of dismantling essentialist beliefs within biology courses. In the future, discussing and dismantling essentialist misconceptions may become an integral part of biology course and program learning goals.

### so, what work needs to be done?
<hr>
If we know that essentialist beliefs are barriers to student learning gains in the biological sciences, and experts are developing curricular scaffolding to address misconceptions in class, is it only a matter of time until widespread adoption of new, counter-essentialist pedagogical practices? It's possible, but the connection between biologically essentialist beliefs and contemporary social controversies complicates this task for instructors. 

Across STEM disciplines, instructors hesitate to teach content they perceive as ideological, often out of fear of disrupting the value-free ideal of science. Many instructors were themselves taught that STEM classrooms are not appropriate places to discuss contemporary social issues, even when those issues are directly informed by scientific consensus. In addition to topics related to biological essentialism, this wariness can extend to any topic where biology curriculum interacts with their student's ideological perspectives. Ultimately, biology instructors seem to expend substantial energy trying to separate social conversations of climate change, religion, race, gender, and ability from scientific conversations about ecology, evolution, genetics, and physiology.

My research is uncovering how instructors often rely on intentional objectivity and the value-free ideal to build *ethos* with their students, and why that may cause them to be wary about discuss certain topics in class. I interviewed college biology instructors who expressed that their decision whether to teach content related to contemporary controversies involved consideration of multiple factors that were influenced by their personal and professional context. They expressed a variety of perspectives on which topics they were willing to approach in class, and how they were willing (or not) to introduce those topics to their students.

Now, my future research will develop our understanding of the factors that affect instructor's course content decisions surrounding essentialism, ideology, and social controversy. Although this work is ongoing, my goal is to uncover and excavate any baggage preventing biology instructors from completely passing their knowledge and expertise to their students. After all, biology students are in our classes to learn biology! In order to equip them with accurate and complete educations, instructors must feel empowered to share facts and evidence without worry.
